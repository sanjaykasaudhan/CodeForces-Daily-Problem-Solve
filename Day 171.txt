✅ Accepted on Codeforces! Problem B — Lasers | Round 1050 (Div. 4)

A physics-inspired problem with a beautifully counter-intuitive solution — the actual laser positions are completely irrelevant! 🔦🎯

🔗Problem Link: https://lnkd.in/gNMpDh4v

🧩 The Problem: You have two walls with n mirrors on the left wall and m mirrors on the right wall. A laser bounces between walls hitting mirrors. Given the coordinates of mirrors on each wall (and the laser entry/exit points x and y), find the minimum number of mirrors the laser must hit!
The code reads n, m, x, y... then reads two more lines (the actual mirror positions!) and discards them entirely with just input(). Then outputs n + m! 😮
The answer is always n + m! 🎯

💡 The Mathematical Insight:
No matter where the mirrors are positioned on each wall, a laser bouncing between two parallel walls must hit every single mirror on both walls before exiting (due to the geometry of reflections)!
This is a classic result from optics and geometry: in a system of two parallel reflective walls, a laser beam that enters and exits will necessarily interact with ALL mirrors present, regardless of their positions!
Therefore:
Minimum mirrors hit = n + m
The positions x and y of entry/exit, and the actual mirror coordinates, are ALL irrelevant! 🤯

Examples:
n=1, m=1, ... → 1+1=2 ✅
n=2, m=1, ... → 2+1=3 ✅

📌 Key Takeaways:
🔦 Sometimes ALL the input is a red herring — reading x, y, and mirror positions but discarding them entirely is the correct approach! The answer depends only on n and m! 🎯
🔦 Physics/geometry theorems collapse complex problems — the reflection theorem that forces a laser to hit all mirrors reduces a seemingly complex optimization to a trivial sum! ✅
🔦 input() as a discard operation — when you need to skip input lines in Python, bare input() without assignment is the clean way! 🐍
🔦 Read ALL the input — even if you discard it, failing to consume all input lines causes runtime errors on subsequent test cases! Always read what the problem gives you! ⚠️
