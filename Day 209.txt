This problem initially seemed to require searching for the smallest integer k satisfying a divisibility condition. However, the key insight was that the answer depends entirely on the prime factorization of n, turning the problem into a neat number theory exercise. 💡

🔗Problem Link: https://lnkd.in/g8ENdWFV

🔍 Problem Understanding
We are given an integer n and need to find the smallest positive integer k such that:
n∣knn \mid k^nn∣knIn other words, n must divide knk^nkn.

🧠 Key Observation
Express n in its prime factorization:
n=p1a1×p2a2×⋯×pmamn = p_1^{a_1} \times p_2^{a_2} \times \cdots \times p_m^{a_m}n=p1a1​​×p2a2​​×⋯×pmam​​For knk^nkn to be divisible by n, every prime factor of n must also appear in k.
Suppose:
n = 12 = 2² × 3

Then k must contain both prime factors:
k = 2 × 3 = 6

Checking:
6126^{12}612contains far more than enough powers of both 2 and 3, so it is divisible by 12.
Therefore, the smallest valid k is simply the product of all distinct prime factors of n.
This value is also known as the square-free kernel (or radical) of n.

⚡ Approach
1️⃣ Factorize n.
2️⃣ For every distinct prime factor, multiply it into the answer.
3️⃣ Ignore repeated occurrences of the same prime.
Example:
n = 360

360 = 2³ × 3² × 5

Answer:
k = 2 × 3 × 5 = 30

💻 Core Implementation

ans = 1

for each distinct prime factor p:
    ans *= p

print(ans)

📈 Complexity Analysis
Time Complexity: O(√n) (trial division factorization)
Space Complexity: O(1)
Efficient enough for the given constraints.

✅ Mathematical observations often eliminate the need for brute-force searching.
