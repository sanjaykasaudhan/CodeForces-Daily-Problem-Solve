🧩 The Problem: You're given an n × m grid filled with . and #. A Manhattan Circle of radius r centred at (cx, cy) covers all cells where the Manhattan distance from the centre is ≤ r. You're told the grid contains exactly one such Manhattan Circle somewhere. Find its centre (cx, cy)! 🎯

💡 My Approach — Row Max + Column Max:
The key geometric insight about Manhattan Circles:
🔑 The centre row has the most # cells of any row — because the widest horizontal cross-section passes through the centre!
🔑 Among all rows, the centre column has the most # cells in its column — because the tallest vertical cross-section passes through the centre!
So the algorithm is elegantly simple:
Step 1 — Find best_row:
Count # in each row
The row with the maximum count is the centre row ✅
Step 2 — Find best_col:
Count # in each column across the entire grid
The column with the maximum count is the centre column ✅
Step 3 — Output (best_row + 1, best_col + 1) 🎯

📌 Key Takeaways:
🔷 Geometry helps! The Manhattan Circle is symmetric — its widest row AND tallest column both pass through the centre. This symmetry is the entire key to the solution.
🔷 Decompose axes independently — instead of searching a 2D space, find the best row and best column separately. This reduces O(n²) search to O(n + m)!
🔷 Max counting is powerful — sometimes complex geometry reduces to a simple argmax operation. Always ask: "What's special about the centre compared to other cells?" 🧠

🤯 The "Aha!" Moment: Once you realise the Manhattan Circle looks like a diamond shape ♦️ — and that its widest horizontal slice and tallest vertical slice both pass through the centre — the rest is just counting!
