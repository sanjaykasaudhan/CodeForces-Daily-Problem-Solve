🚀 Accepted on Codeforces! | Problem B – Palindrome, Twelve and Two Terms 🎯

Happy to share another Accepted ✅ solution from Codeforces Round 1102 (Div. 2).

This problem looked like a number theory challenge at first, but the trick was to find a small palindrome such that the remaining value becomes divisible by 12. Once the pattern was identified, the solution became surprisingly simple. 💡

🔗Problem Link: https://lnkd.in/gtbGujnn

🔍 Problem Statement
We need to find two non-negative integers:
a (must be a palindrome)
b (must be divisible by 12)
such that:
a+b=na + b = na+b=nIf no such pair exists, print -1.

🧠 Key Observation
Since b must be divisible by 12:
b=n−ab = n - ab=n−aTherefore:
(n−a) mod
 12=0(n - a) \bmod 12 = 0(n−a)mod12=0which means:
a≡n(mod12)a \equiv n \pmod{12}a≡n(mod12)So, we only need a small palindrome whose value has the same remainder as n modulo 12.

💡 Finding the Palindrome
Let:
r = n % 12

Case 1: r ≤ 9
Single-digit numbers are already palindromes.
a = r

Example:
n = 25
r = 1

a = 1
b = 24

✅ 1 is a palindrome
 ✅ 24 is divisible by 12
Case 2: r = 10
There is no one-digit palindrome equal to 10.
Use:
a = 22

because:
22 % 12 = 10

Case 3: r = 11
Use:
a = 11

which is already a palindrome and:
11 % 12 = 11

Special Case
For:
n = 10

No valid pair exists because every palindrome matching the required remainder exceeds n.
Therefore:
Answer = -1

⚡ Final Approach
1️⃣ Compute:

r = n % 12

2️⃣ Choose the smallest palindrome with remainder r.
3️⃣ Output:

a, n - a

4️⃣ Handle n = 10 separately.
📈 Complexity
Time Complexity: O(1)
Space Complexity: O(1)
A constant-time solution with pure mathematical reasoning. 🚀

🎯 What I Learned
This problem reinforced a valuable competitive programming lesson:
✅ Reduce the problem to modular arithmetic.
✅ Look for the smallest valid construction instead of searching exhaustively.
✅ A clever observation can turn a seemingly difficult problem into an O(1) solution.
